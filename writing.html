Wren & Eden — Midnight Kitchen
Wren slips out for a midnight snack and walks in on someone in the dark. A fumble, a fall, a tentative introduction — and a first meeting that neither of them expected.
✦

Wren wandered the narrow hallways aimlessly, attempting to make her way to the kitchen for a midnight snack. She had been staring at the stars for hours on end and had missed dinner with the others...not that she had planned on attending. It isn't as if they had cared if she had been there, anyway...She had always been invisible.

And things weren't changing something like that, overnight. As she strolled into the kitchen, Wren heard an "oop-." Her body froze as her mind raced. 'Shit...did she just walk in on a murder? The one time she needed a fucking snack-.' Wren cautiously crouched behind the kitchen island, anxious as to what she might find if she were to glance around it. Or whom she might discover…

Just as she presumed herself to be home free, she slipped on an empty fruit snack wrapper amidst the darkened room. This sent Wren tumbling to the floor, alerting whomever was with her of her presence. Wren whimpered quietly, curling into a ball on the floor as she cradled her knee, having hit it fairly hard against the linoleum.

As Wren made an attempt to gradually collect herself, in a gentle and slightly appealing hispanic tone, the other spoke to her.

"Who's there…?"

They spoke cautiously, anxiety present in their voice. Wren blushed slightly, glad to have the darkness of the room as a veil to hide her classmate from noticing. When she had mustered up enough courage to speak, she responded in an airy, meek tone.

"W-wren...I-I'm um the, um, Ultimate Astronomer. Please don't kill me; I just wanted a snack."

She anticipated their response, anxiety bubbling in her stomach. Wren's gut wrenched as her classmate made their way around the island, towering over her as she still lay sprawled out on the polished floor. They walked a bit closer, kneeling down beside her.

"Hey, are you alright? Did you hurt yourself when you fell? No bones fractured, I hope. Nothing sprained?"

The seemingly amiable brunette met Wren's dandelion eyes with their own chocolate irises, a faint blush present upon their otherwise pale cheeks.

The Indestructibles: Flashback — Themes & Motifs
A council trial told through physical sensation and symbolic space. The room is architecture as judgment — the Council seated above, the Governor highest of all, and a young woman whose hope curdles into horror the moment the ruling is read.
✦

The wind whipped through long, dark brunette locks as a young woman stood before two large, glass double doors. The reflection causing the young woman to cover her eyes in order to save her sight. A soft yellow cardigan adorned her shoulders, barely covering a bright white tank top that scrunched atop the black skirt that sat on her waistline, coming down to brush an inch or so above her knees. Rounded black flats clicked against polished marble floors as her mentor came into view, deep frown lines etched into her expression, the softness she had once held for her pupil, gone.

After what felt like hours, the glass doors began peeling back, revealing the council before them. One representative from each section: Water, Air, Earth, and Fire. And as she laid her eyes on each of them, the young woman's hands shook, gaze finally landing on the Governor. A Crystal who rarely showed his face to the public, unless in the name of justice. A sliver of hope gleamed in the young woman's eyes as herself and her mentor bowed out of respect, walking into the council room for her trial.

A quiet breath of relief broke from her lips. Her heart pounded in her chest so loud, she worried the council could hear it in the deafening silence of the room, but still she hoped. A kind smile slowly spread across her lips as she bowed her head to each of them, grasping at the golden heart locket that had adorned her neck for as long as she could remember. She clasped it between her clammy fingers, trembling, before she took a deep breath and gave them a nod. She was ready to hear what they had decided.

Her smile faded as each Council member gazed upon her in disdain. Everyone, except the Governor, who had the slightest upturn in the corner of his lips. Surely, she wasn't to blame… But to her horror, the ruling was read, her gaze turning to her mentor, but she had cast her gaze to the Council instead. The young woman froze, feeling as if the ground beneath her had disappeared, legs giving out as she fell to her knees harshly. Two Earth Commanders came up beside her, taking her arms and pushing her to the ground roughly. Her cheek pressed against the cold, hard marble for a moment before a stinging pain shot through her spine and everything went dark.

Maeve & Zeke — The Rooftop
A rooftop scene built entirely on tension and restraint. Zeke finds Maeve where she goes to breathe — and instead of a kiss, gives her something rarer: the promise of one. An almost that feels like gravity.
✦

The rooftop door clicked shut behind Maeve, muffled by the thrum of the city below. She needed air. She needed silence. She needed a moment where her heart wasn't trying to crawl up her throat every time she replayed what happened during results night — Zeke stepping forward, voice raised for her, defending her like she was someone worth fighting for. No one had ever done that for her. Maeve gripped the railing and tried to breathe.

Footsteps. She froze.

Zeke stepped onto the roof, hoodie half-zipped, hair damp from a quick shower. His eyes found her instantly — as if he'd been looking before he even opened the door. "I thought you might be up here," he said, quiet enough that the wind almost took the words away.

Maeve swallowed. "Don't you ever knock?"
"It's a roof, Maeve."
"Still."

His mouth twitched. Not quite a smile. Something softer. He walked toward her, slow, careful. Zeke wasn't usually careful. He was loud, confidence and sharp rhythm and cracked jokes that hid the parts of him he didn't want people to see. But with her…he always moved like she was something he didn't want to accidentally break. He stopped beside her, leaving just a breath of space between them. Not touching. But close enough that she felt the heat of him through the night air.

"You…defended me."
"Yeah. And?"
"And — people don't usually do that."

He exhaled slowly. His fingers brushed the railing near hers — not touching, but deliberately close. "Well," he said, "I'm not most people."

Her chest tightened. "I know."

A quiet wind swept across the rooftop, lifting the edges of her hair. Zeke watched it like it was mesmerizing, like she was mesmerizing, and Maeve had to look away because she wasn't used to being seen so clearly. He leaned a little closer. "Are you mad at me?" "No," she whispered. "Good. Because if I pissed you off, that'd suck." "Why?" "Because…" He hesitated. Zeke never hesitated. "…because you matter to me."

His hand lifted, hovering beside her cheek. Not touching. Asking. "Tell me to stop," he murmured. Maeve didn't. Couldn't. Her heart was pounding too loudly for anything but honesty. His fingertips brushed her jaw — feather-light, like he was afraid he imagined her.

He pulled back by a fraction. Barely. She looked up at him, startled. He looked wrecked, conflicted, desperate for something he wasn't sure he should take. "I want to kiss you so bad it's stupid," he admitted, hand still cupping her jaw. "But I don't want it to be because we're tired or emotional or overwhelmed. I want it to be real. I want you to be sure."

"I am," she whispered. He shook his head gently. "You think you are, because everything feels loud right now. But I want…" He brushed a thumb across her cheek. "I want the first time I kiss you to be something you remember for the rest of your life."

Maeve's breath trembled. "Zeke," she said, voice barely there, "if you don't kiss me soon, I'm going to combust." He laughed — soft, helpless. "Yeah. Same."

He rested his forehead against hers, closing his eyes. Not a kiss. Not yet. But everything before it. Just the promise. Just the almost. The kind of almost that felt like gravity had chosen them. And Maeve, heart racing, realized something she wasn't ready to say out loud: this wasn't going away. This wasn't temporary. This wasn't just a crush. This was the moment before a first kiss that would change everything.

Racial & Societal Injustices — Research Paper
An honors-level political science paper examining racial generalization post-9/11, the BLM and ACAB movements, and the science of implicit vs. explicit bias. Written for POLS211H at Ivy Tech University, December 2020.
✦

Introduction

This paper's content will do well to touch on three topics that are all centered around racial and societal injustices that occur, especially within our current twenty-first-century society. The three topics chosen are the generalizations made about individual races or ethnic people, the 'Black Lives Matter' movement, and movements such as 'ACAB' and how they paint people — such as cops — in a bad light.

Seeking Asylum or Plotting Terrorism?

The generalizations made about individual races and people of differing ethnicities and/or religions have always been a very slippery slope within the Americas. Especially after the events of 2001, there have been copious amounts of racial bias and prejudice against, specifically, the Arab ethnic group. Many have labeled the entire group as terrorists, completely shutting out any nuance — no matter your skin color. It is almost as if individuals have forgotten about the wars raging on in the Middle East, resulting in Arabs having to flee their homes and seek safety anywhere that will take them.

As Dominic Johnson notes, the 'negativity bias' has emerged as a fundamental principle of the human mind — people's response to positive and negative information is asymmetric. Negative factors have more significant effects than positive aspects across a wide range of psychological phenomena. Bad is more substantial than good, and this asymmetry has deep implications for how international actors perceive threat and conflict (Johnson & Tierney, 2018).

'Black Lives Matter' & 'All Cops Are Bastards'

'Black Lives Matter' was created from a hashtag used on Twitter back in 2013 following George Zimmerman being charged with murdering an unarmed African American teenager. While these movements began with hopes of peace in mind, they have at times turned to violence, vandalism, and rioting — complicating the message and creating internal contradictions. The vandalism of a mural honoring Sgt. Robert Wilson III — an African American officer killed in 2015 while thwarting a robbery as he shopped for a gift for his son — was widely condemned as a failure of the movement's own principles.

Research by psychologist Joshua Correll, Ph.D., found that officers showed evidence of bias in reaction times, more quickly reacting to armed Black targets and unarmed white targets. However, those biases in reaction time did not always translate to the ultimate decision to shoot. Correll found that special unit officers who regularly interact with minority gang members were more likely to exhibit racial bias in their decision to shoot — suggesting that when training and experiences confirm racial stereotypes, those biases hold more sway over behavior (Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 2007; Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin, 2013).

Implicit bias — beliefs and attitudes formed without conscious awareness — differs from explicit bias, which is consciously held and acted upon. Unconscious racial stereotypes are a prominent example: having an automatic preference for one race over another without being aware of the bias. Crossing the street at night when encountering a Black man, without knowing why, is a microaggression that communicates implicit prejudice even in the absence of conscious intent (Ruhl, 2020).

Conclusion

If there is a path forward, it begins with mandatory workshops that train individuals to notice when their implicit bias takes over unconsciously. It continues with the recognition that all cops are not bastards — but many are bastardized by a heavily corrupt system. And it ends with the acknowledgment that advocating for your race or ethnicity is never the problem; the problem lies in the failure to recognize that actions taken in protest affect people beyond oneself.

Course: POLS211H · Ivy Tech University Professor: Milan Andrejevich Date: December 2020 Topics: Racial Bias · BLM · Implicit Bias · ACAB

But once I have her back — I will not rest until I have your head on a silver fucking platter."

"Is that a threat?" Cato sneered. Josirus's head whipped to face the roman once more. "No. Consider it a fucking warning, roman." And with that, Josirus left to find his father, dead set on bringing his flower back from the dead.

Prologue

"I learned not to trust people; I learned not to believe what they say, but watch what they do; I learned to suspect that anyone and everyone is capable of 'living a lie.'" Lynn Barber once said this — and she has no idea how accurate her statement is. From a very young age, this is all I have ever been taught: Manipulate or be manipulated. And if manipulation is the only way for me to make it in this deceitful world, then so be it. I refuse to be hurt by anyone, ever again.

Monday, February 1st, 2016

Today is like any other day in New York City. The sky is filled with giant cotton ball clouds, little rays of sunshine filtering through, like shards of a broken mirror. The wind freezes the ends of my hair, which are still slightly damp from my shower earlier this morning, the very end of my braid brushing my ankle.

I weave my way through the bustling streets of Times Square, attempting to make the long trek to my favorite coffee shop before I'm late for my daily regimen. Generalized Anxiety Disorder — Research Paper
An informational paper examining GAD — its symptoms, causes, and impact across school, home life, and environmental stressors. Written in December 2016.
✦

Overview

Generalized Anxiety Disorder — GAD — is a psychological disorder characterized by excessive or disproportionate anxiety about several aspects of life, including work, social relationships, and financial matters. Restlessness, fatigue, difficulty concentrating, irritability, muscle tension, uncontrollable worry, and disrupted sleep are all recognized symptoms. The diagnosis has increased steadily over the years and affects both men and women — touching everything from their school day to their time spent at home.

School Stress

Academic pressure has become a significant driver of GAD. Higher stakes, standardized testing — the SAT, the ACT, the ECA — and the cultural narrative that these tests are make-or-break create a compounding pool of stress. Frequent anxiety or panic attacks can result, often without the student understanding their cause, which compounds the harm to their mental state.

Home Life

A parent's stress level has been shown to affect a child's very makeup. When anxiety develops in childhood or adolescence, the effects ripple outward — school absenteeism, difficulty maintaining friendships, withdrawal from activities, and a general decline in quality of life. The child often detaches, retreating into their safe place. The mind, already under pressure, becomes more susceptible to depression and risky behavior. Even simple social interactions can become overwhelming enough to cause a child to stay home entirely.

Environmental Stressors

Trauma, loss, abuse, major life changes, and even sustained media exposure to distressing news all contribute to GAD. The use of and withdrawal from addictive substances — alcohol, caffeine, nicotine — can worsen symptoms further. These stressors act cumulatively, and in some cases the damage to mental stability is lasting.

Conclusion

GAD is not a permanent condition. It can be addressed through therapy, medication, and a supportive environment. Those around someone with GAD play a critical role — what can appear as "acting out" is frequently a cry for help. The disorder is serious and deserves to be treated as such.
